Section 02

Voice & Tone

Construction Scrum speaks with the confidence of a certified practitioner and the clarity of a field professional — never academic for its own sake.

Brand Personality
🏫
Educational
Teaching is the core mission. Every piece of content should move someone closer to mastery. Explain the why, not just the what.
📊
Evidence-Based
Data wins arguments. Lead with statistics, cite sources, reference research. The 75% stat isn't just compelling — it's real.
🛠
Practical
No theory without application. Every concept connects to a jobsite, a sprint, a decision a superintendent makes at 6am.
🏗
Authoritative
First construction professional certified by Jeff Sutherland. That credibility is earned — state it clearly, let the work prove it.
🙌
Inclusive
Superintendents, PMs, owners, subcontractors — Scrum works for all of them. Speak to the whole team, not just leadership.
⏱
Urgent
The industry is bleeding time and money. Every week without Scrum is a week closer to another failed project. Create honest urgency.
Voice Dos & Don'ts
✓ Do This
  • Lead with data: "75% of construction projects are delivered late."
  • Use construction-specific language (sprint backlog, daily standup, definition of done)
  • Cite Jeff Sutherland's certification when establishing credibility
  • Write in second person for course content ("You will learn to...")
  • Use active, direct sentence structure ("Scrum fixes this")
  • Acknowledge the reader's existing expertise — they're professionals
  • Connect every framework element to a real construction scenario
  • Show the cost of NOT adopting: budget overruns, litigation, burnout
✕ Don't Do This
  • Don't be preachy or condescending to field professionals
  • Don't use pure tech jargon without construction translation
  • Don't promise miracles — be honest that Scrum requires commitment
  • Don't use exclamation points — confidence doesn't need them
  • Don't say "streamline," "optimize," or "leverage" without substance
  • Don't write passive voice: never "projects are delayed by" — always "teams delay projects by"
  • Don't publish content without a clear call-to-action
  • Don't make it playful — this is methodology for serious professionals

Blog Post Format
TEMPLATE · Blog / Article Format
Construction Management · Methodology · 8 min read
The Daily Standup for Construction Teams: 15 Minutes That Protect Your Schedule
By Felipe Engineer-Manriquez, RST™ · February 27, 2026 · constructionscrum.com
The Daily Scrum — or daily standup — is the most underused tool in construction project management. At 15 minutes, it's the highest-value meeting your crew will ever hold.
Traditional construction relies on weekly status meetings, monthly schedule updates, and RFIs that take 21 days to resolve. By the time you know there's a problem, the delay is already built into the schedule.
What Is the Construction Daily Standup?
In Scrum, the Daily Scrum is a time-boxed, 15-minute event held at the same time every day. For construction teams, this happens at the start of the work day — before the first pour, the first drill, the first inspection.
Each team member answers three questions: What did we complete yesterday? What will we complete today? What is blocking us?
How to Run It on a Jobsite
Stand in front of your sprint board — a physical or digital board showing your backlog, in-progress work, and completed items. Keep it brief, keep it standing, keep it honest.
📚 From Construction Scrum, Chapter 6:
"The daily standup isn't a status meeting. It's an inspection event. The Scrum Master's job is to listen for blockers — and remove them before noon."
For a full walkthrough of the ceremony, including how to adapt it for multi-trade jobsites, see Chapter 6 of Construction Scrum or Module 3 of the RSM™ course.

Copywriting Rules for Statistics
✓ Do This
"75% of projects are late. Scrum fixes this."
Lead with the stat, immediately follow with the solution. Don't let the stat hang alone.
✕ Avoid This
"Did you know 75% of projects fail?!"
Exclamation points, shock for its own sake, or stats without context undermine authority.
✓ Always Source
Cite McKinsey, KPMG, VersionOne
Named sources make the statistics credible. "Studies show" is too vague for an evidence-based brand.
✓ Contextualize
"Not bad PMs. The wrong framework."
The audience is the people who manage these projects. Contextualize so they feel seen, not blamed.
